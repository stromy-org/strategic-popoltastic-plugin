# Announcement Types

Detailed guidance per announcement type — what to emphasize, key elements, common pitfalls, and example leads. Load this file when drafting type-specific content (Phase 4).

---

## Product Launch

**Classification**: Usually Ordinary. Elevated if it's a regulated product (medical device, financial product) or replaces a widely-used existing product.

### Key Elements
- What the product does (function, not marketing)
- Who it's for (target audience/market)
- When it's available (date, phased rollout, waitlist)
- Pricing or pricing model (if public)
- How to access it (URL, app store, sales contact)
- One concrete metric or proof point (beta results, performance benchmark)

### What to Emphasize
The problem it solves and for whom — not the feature list. Journalists need to explain to their readers why this matters. Lead with the user benefit, not the technology.

### Common Pitfalls
- Feature dumping — listing 15 features instead of leading with the one that matters
- "Revolutionary" / "game-changing" without evidence
- No availability date or access information
- Burying the price (if it's a strength) or avoiding it entirely (readers notice)

### Example Lead
"**SAN FRANCISCO, March 15, 2027** — Acme Software today launched DataPro 3.0, an analytics platform that reduces financial reporting time by 80 percent for mid-market retailers. The platform is available immediately, starting at $500 per month."

## Funding / Investment

**Classification**: Market-sensitive if the company is publicly traded or the round is significant enough to affect existing investors. Otherwise Ordinary.

### Key Elements
- Amount raised
- Round type (Seed, Series A/B/C, debt, etc.)
- Lead investor(s)
- Notable participating investors
- Intended use of funds (hiring, expansion, R&D, specific geography)
- Current business metrics that justify the raise (ARR, customer count, growth rate)

### What to Emphasize
What the money enables, not that money was raised. Investors are a means; the story is the company's trajectory.

### Common Pitfalls
- Leading with the investor name instead of the company's achievement
- No concrete use-of-funds — "fuel growth" is meaningless
- Missing valuation context (if disclosable and advantageous)
- No business metrics to anchor the raise — why did investors say yes?

### Example Lead
"**AUSTIN, Texas, March 15, 2027** — TechStart, a developer tools company serving 50,000 teams, today announced $25 million in Series B funding led by Sequoia Capital. The company will use the funds to open engineering offices in London and Berlin and double its enterprise sales team."

## Partnership / Alliance

**Classification**: Usually Ordinary. Market-sensitive if it involves a material commercial agreement, exclusive arrangement, or changes to either company's market position.

### Key Elements
- Both parties and what each brings
- What the partnership enables that neither could do alone
- Customer or market benefit
- Scope (exclusive/non-exclusive, geography, duration)
- Availability or timeline
- Quotes from both organizations

### What to Emphasize
The customer outcome. A partnership between two companies is only news if it changes something for the market.

### Common Pitfalls
- Vague partnership with no concrete deliverable ("will explore opportunities")
- No customer benefit articulated
- Only one company's perspective (get quotes from both)
- "Strategic partnership" without saying what's strategic about it

### Example Lead
"**NEW YORK, March 15, 2027** — Acme Corp and GlobalTech today announced a partnership to deliver integrated supply chain forecasting for enterprise manufacturers. The solution combines Acme's logistics platform with GlobalTech's AI prediction engine and will be available to joint customers in Q3 2027."

## Acquisition / M&A

**Classification**: Almost always Market-sensitive. Legal and IR review mandatory.

### Key Elements
- Acquirer and target
- Transaction value (if disclosable)
- Strategic rationale — why this acquisition, why now
- Expected close date and conditions
- Impact on customers of both companies
- Impact on employees
- Regulatory approvals needed
- Quotes from leadership of both companies

### What to Emphasize
Strategic rationale and customer continuity. Customers of the acquired company want to know their service is safe. The market wants to know why this makes the acquirer stronger.

### Common Pitfalls
- No mention of customer impact — breeds uncertainty
- Forward-looking synergy claims without evidence
- Ignoring the target company's identity — it feels like erasure
- Not addressing regulatory/closing conditions
- Missing the "subject to customary closing conditions" language

### Legal Requirements
- Forward-looking statement disclaimer (mandatory)
- "Subject to customary closing conditions" (mandatory)
- No specific synergy dollar amounts unless approved by both boards
- Coordinate with both companies' legal teams on every draft

### Example Lead
"**LONDON, March 15, 2027** — Acme Corp today announced it has agreed to acquire DataCorp, a machine learning infrastructure company, for $180 million in cash. The acquisition strengthens Acme's AI capabilities and is expected to close in Q2 2027, subject to regulatory approval."

## Executive Appointment / Departure

**Classification**: Ordinary for most hires. Market-sensitive if it's a CEO, CFO, or Board member at a listed company.

### Key Elements
- Name and new title
- Effective date
- Background (most recent role, relevant experience, education if notable)
- What they'll be responsible for
- Why now (growth, new capability, succession)
- Quote from the hiring executive and optionally from the new hire
- For departures: reason (if disclosable), interim arrangement, successor timeline

### What to Emphasize
What this person will do, not just who they are. A bio is not news. The news is the capability or direction the hire represents.

### Common Pitfalls
- Pure biography with no strategic context
- "We are thrilled to welcome..." (pure boilerplate)
- No effective date
- For departures: vague language that invites speculation

### Example Lead
"**SAN FRANCISCO, March 15, 2027** — Acme Software today appointed Jane Smith as Chief Technology Officer, effective April 1. Smith joins from Google, where she led the Cloud AI engineering team, and will oversee Acme's product development and AI strategy."

## Company Milestone

**Classification**: Usually Ordinary. Market-sensitive if the milestone involves financial metrics at a listed company.

### Key Elements
- The specific milestone (number, achievement, ranking)
- Context — growth trajectory, time to reach it, industry comparison
- What it means for customers or the market
- What's next

### What to Emphasize
Significance, not the number in isolation. "10,000 customers" is a fact. "10,000 customers in 18 months, making Acme the fastest-growing SaaS platform in European retail" is a story.

### Common Pitfalls
- No context — the number means nothing without a benchmark
- "Vanity metrics" that don't reflect business health
- No forward-looking element — what does this milestone enable?
- Celebrating an internal milestone that doesn't matter externally

### Example Lead
"**AMSTERDAM, March 15, 2027** — Acme Consulting today announced it has surpassed 10,000 enterprise customers across 40 countries, tripling its customer base in two years. The milestone reflects growing demand for AI-driven operational consulting among European manufacturers."

## Research / Report / Survey

**Classification**: Usually Ordinary. Market-sensitive if findings could affect investor expectations.

### Key Elements
- Top-line finding (the one headline-worthy insight)
- Methodology (sample size, population, method, date)
- 2-3 supporting data points
- What the findings mean for the industry
- Where to access the full report
- Who conducted the research (company, third party, jointly)

### What to Emphasize
The finding, not the fact that research was done. Lead with the insight, not "Today released a report."

### Common Pitfalls
- Leading with "Company X today released..." instead of the finding
- No methodology transparency — undermines credibility
- Cherry-picking only favorable results
- Confusing correlation with causation in the headline

### Example Lead
"**NEW YORK, March 15, 2027** — Seventy-eight percent of CFOs at Fortune 500 companies plan to increase AI investment by more than 25 percent in 2027, according to a new study by Acme Research. The survey of 500 senior finance executives found that cost reduction and forecasting accuracy are the top AI priorities."

## Crisis Communication

**Classification**: Always Crisis. Full governance applies.

### Key Elements
- What happened (facts only, confirmed information)
- Who is affected
- What the company is doing about it (specific actions, not platitudes)
- What affected parties should do (concrete steps)
- Timeline for next update
- How to get help (hotline, website, contact)

### What to Emphasize
Facts, actions, and empathy — in that order. Do not speculate. Do not minimize. Do not blame.

### The Crisis Release is Different

Unlike other release types, crisis communications:
- **Start with a holding statement** before the full release
- **Update frequently** with new information
- **Never go silent** — commit to an update schedule and keep it
- **Prioritize affected parties** over media
- **Are reviewed by legal at every version**

### Holding Statement Template
"[Company] is aware of [brief description of incident]. We are [specific immediate action]. The safety/security/wellbeing of [affected parties] is our top priority. We will provide an update by [specific time]. For immediate assistance, contact [hotline/email]."

### Common Pitfalls
- Saying "We take this very seriously" without demonstrating it with actions
- Speculating on cause before investigation is complete
- Minimizing the impact
- Blaming others before understanding the facts
- Going silent after the initial statement
- Using legal language that sounds evasive rather than transparent

### Tone Guidance
- **Use**: "We are addressing," "We have taken [specific action]," "We are committed to [specific outcome]"
- **Avoid**: "We apologize for any inconvenience" (minimizing), "This was an isolated incident" (before you know), "We take this seriously" (overused, meaningless)

### Example Holding Statement
"**SAN FRANCISCO, March 15, 2027** — Acme Software is responding to a data security incident that may have affected customer account information. We have engaged a leading cybersecurity firm and notified law enforcement. Affected customers are being contacted directly with steps to protect their accounts. We will provide a full update by 5:00 PM PT today. Customers with questions can contact our dedicated support line at 1-800-555-0199."

## Type Selection Quick Reference

| If the user says... | Type | Key reference |
|--------------------|------|---------------|
| "We're launching a product" | Product Launch | Features → benefits, availability, pricing |
| "We closed a funding round" | Funding | Amount, lead investor, use of funds |
| "We're partnering with X" | Partnership | Customer benefit, both perspectives |
| "We're acquiring / being acquired" | Acquisition | Rationale, customer impact, legal language |
| "We hired a new [executive]" | Executive Appointment | Strategic context, responsibilities |
| "We hit [number] customers/users" | Milestone | Context, trajectory, significance |
| "Our research shows..." | Research/Report | Finding first, methodology, full report link |
| "There's been an incident" | Crisis | Facts, actions, next update, help line |
