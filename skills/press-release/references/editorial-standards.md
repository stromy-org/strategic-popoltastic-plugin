# Editorial Standards

AP-style editorial conventions for corporate press releases. Load this file when drafting (Phase 4).

---

## Headline Rules

The headline is the single most important line. A reader should understand the news without opening the email.

**Formula**: `[Subject] + [Action Verb] + [Object/Outcome]`

| Rule | Guidance |
|------|---------|
| Length | 6-12 words, under 100 characters |
| Voice | Active, present tense |
| Case | Title case |
| Specificity | State the news — not a teaser, not a question |
| Keywords | Include the company name and the core news element |
| Avoid | Superlatives, exclamation points, "announces" (it's weak), all caps, jargon |
| Punctuation | No period at the end |

### Examples by Type

| Type | Headline |
|------|---------|
| Product | Acme Launches AI Analytics Platform for Mid-Market Retailers |
| Funding | TechStart Raises $25M Series B to Expand European Operations |
| Partnership | Acme and Microsoft Partner on Enterprise Supply Chain Solutions |
| Acquisition | Acme Acquires DataCorp to Strengthen Machine Learning Capabilities |
| Executive | Jane Smith Joins Acme as Chief Technology Officer |
| Milestone | Acme Surpasses 10,000 Enterprise Customers Globally |
| Research | New Study: 78% of CFOs Plan AI Investment Increase in 2027 |
| Crisis | Acme Addresses Data Security Incident, Outlines Customer Protections |

### When to Use a Subheadline

Add a subheadline when:
- The headline needs a supporting metric ("Platform reduces reporting time by 80%")
- The news has two distinct angles
- The announcement is complex enough that one line can't convey the full picture

Keep subheadlines under 120 characters.

## Lead Paragraph

The lead is the release in miniature. If a journalist reads nothing else, they should have the full story.

**Requirements:**
- 25-30 words (35 absolute maximum)
- Answers: Who, What, When, Where, Why
- Starts with the dateline: `**CITY, Month Day, Year** —`
- Does not start with "We are thrilled/excited/pleased to announce..."
- Uses active voice and concrete language

**Template:**
`**[CITY], [Month Day, Year]** — [Company Name], [brief descriptor], today [action verb] [the news], [key benefit or impact]. [Supporting detail or availability.]`

### Lead Examples

**Product Launch:**
"**SAN FRANCISCO, January 18, 2027** — Acme Software, a business analytics company, today launched DataPro 3.0, an AI platform that cuts reporting time by 80%. The new version is available immediately for enterprise customers."

**Funding:**
"**AUSTIN, Texas, January 18, 2027** — TechStart, a developer tools provider, today announced $25 million in Series B funding led by Sequoia Capital. The company will use the funds to accelerate product development and enter three new European markets."

## Quote Guidelines

Quotes exist to add what facts cannot: perspective, strategic intent, and human voice. A quote that restates the headline is wasted space.

### Rules

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Add perspective the facts don't convey | Repeat the headline or lead |
| Sound like a real person speaking | Use corporate jargon or buzzwords |
| Include forward-looking context | Start with "I" |
| Keep to 2-3 sentences | Write paragraph-length quotes |
| Attribute with full name and title | Use "thrilled," "excited," "delighted" |

### Quote Formula

`"[Statement about significance or vision]. [Company commitment or action]. [Forward-looking implication for customers/market]," said [Full Name], [Title] at [Company].`

### Attribution Format

AP style: `"Quote," said Full Name, Title at Company.`
- Use "said" — not "stated," "commented," "noted," or "shared"
- Title before name only if it's a common title (President Jane Smith); otherwise name first
- For very long titles, put the title after the name

### When to Use Two Quotes

Use a second quote when:
- A partner or customer provides independent validation
- The announcement involves two organizations (partnership, acquisition)
- An analyst or industry figure adds credibility

The second quote should not echo the first — each must contribute something distinct.

## AP Style Quick Reference

### Numbers
- Spell out one through nine; use digits for 10 and above
- Always use digits for: ages, percentages, currency, dates, addresses, scores
- Spell out numbers that begin a sentence (or restructure to avoid it)
- "Percent" as one word: "15 percent" (not 15% in body text; % is acceptable in headlines)
- Use "$25 million" not "$25M" in body text

### Dates
- Month abbreviations: Jan., Feb., Aug., Sept., Oct., Nov., Dec.
- March, April, May, June, July are spelled out
- Day format: Jan. 18, 2027 (not January 18th, 2027)
- Omit the year if it's the current year and the context is clear

### Dateline
- Format: `CITY, State/Country, Month Day, Year —`
- U.S. cities: use AP state abbreviations (Calif., Mass., N.Y.) — not postal codes (CA, MA, NY)
- Major standalone cities (no state needed): Atlanta, Baltimore, Boston, Chicago, Cincinnati, Cleveland, Dallas, Denver, Detroit, Honolulu, Houston, Indianapolis, Las Vegas, Los Angeles, Miami, Milwaukee, Minneapolis, New Orleans, New York, Oklahoma City, Philadelphia, Phoenix, Pittsburgh, St. Louis, Salt Lake City, San Antonio, San Diego, San Francisco, Seattle, Washington
- International: `LONDON, Jan. 18, 2027 —` (no country for well-known capitals)

### Titles
- Capitalize formal titles before a name: President Jane Smith
- Lowercase titles after a name: Jane Smith, president of Acme
- Lowercase job descriptions: analyst John Doe
- No courtesy titles (Mr., Ms., Dr.) unless relevant (Dr. for medical/scientific context)

### Company Names
- First reference: full legal name without Inc., Corp., Ltd.
- Subsequent: shortened name or common reference
- No trademark symbols (™, ®) in body text

### Punctuation
- AP uses the serial comma only when needed for clarity (unlike Oxford style)
- One space after periods
- Em dashes with spaces: ` — ` (not `—`)

## Length Guidance

| Element | Target |
|---------|--------|
| Headline | 6-12 words, under 100 characters |
| Subheadline | Under 120 characters (optional) |
| Lead paragraph | 25-30 words |
| Full release | 300-500 words |
| Quotes | 2-3 sentences each, 1-2 quotes total |
| Boilerplate | 2-3 sentences |

**Why 300-500 words?** Current industry consensus (PRSA, PR Newswire) favors concise, digital-first releases. Shorter releases get read; longer ones get skimmed. If the announcement genuinely requires more detail, move supporting information to a linked fact sheet or backgrounder — not into the release body.

If a draft exceeds 500 words, cut by asking of each sentence: "Does this communicate the news, prove it, explain why it matters, or enable follow-up?" If the answer is no, delete it.

## Tone and Language

### Write for Journalists, Not Internal Stakeholders

Journalists want content they can scan, trust, and repurpose. Write as if the text might appear in a news article with minimal editing — because that's the goal.

### Be Precise, Plainspoken, Low-Hype

Avoid:
- "World-leading," "game-changing," "revolutionary," "best-in-class" (unless you can prove it)
- "Solutions" as a standalone noun (solutions to what?)
- "Synergy," "leverage," "ecosystem," "paradigm shift"
- "Pleased/thrilled/excited to announce"
- Passive voice when active is clearer
- Long sentences — if a sentence has more than 25 words, split it

### Conservative Wording for Sensitive Topics

For crisis, legal, regulatory, or market-sensitive announcements, err on the side of precision over punchiness. Reuters' editorial standards emphasize accuracy, fair comment, and transparent correction — this is the right benchmark for sensitive corporate communications.

- State facts, not opinions
- Attribute claims clearly
- Avoid forward-looking language unless approved by legal
- Use measured language — "addressing" not "conquering," "incident" not "attack" (unless confirmed)

## Common Mistakes

These are the errors that most consistently undermine corporate press releases, drawn from PRSA, PR Newswire, Reuters, and AP guidance:

1. **Announcing non-news** — Routine milestones that don't matter externally
2. **Burying the lead** — Key fact in paragraph 3 instead of paragraph 1
3. **Sounding like advertising** — Hype language instead of newsworthy facts
4. **Unsupported superlatives** — "First," "only," "leading" without evidence
5. **Overusing quotes** — More than 2 quotes, or quotes that repeat the facts
6. **Missing evidence** — No numbers, dates, metrics, or specifics
7. **No media contact** — Or a generic inbox instead of a real person
8. **Wrong audience** — Written for the CEO instead of a beat reporter
9. **Too long** — Above 500 words with no fact sheet for the overflow
10. **Formatting errors** — Wrong dateline, inconsistent capitalization, trademark symbols in body
